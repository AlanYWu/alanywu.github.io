- Finished my plant science experiment after a 36-hour push to close out a semester of planning and delays. Months of "next week, next week" finally became a real result. Relieved doesn't begin to cover it.
- Sat down with the first potential customer for the automated financial advising tool I'm building for car dealerships. Two and a half hours of him walking me through how dealerships actually make money, what the financial and accounting reports look like in practice, and where the real margin leaks hide. He offered to keep teaching me the domain, and together we sketched out what a first prototype needs to do.
- The real lesson from this semester wasn't "study harder next time." It was: stop half-engaging. Showing up to a course without actually committing to it costs you the time of being there without producing any of the learning. Better to pick fewer courses and go deep, and treat the rest with an honest minimum, than to spread thin across everything and walk away with not much from any of it.
- Sitting with a bigger question too. What is college actually for? The technical knowledge from advanced courses, the people, the practice at carrying stress, the network you build? If the goal is to found a consumer or hardtech company in a few years, I should be more deliberate about which of those I'm optimizing for, instead of splitting attention across all of them by default.
